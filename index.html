Inside the Ladder and Armendaris Ranches

Spanning vast stretches of southern New Mexico, the Ladder and Armendaris Ranches represent some of the most ambitious conservation and restoration efforts in North America. These expansive wildlands serve as dedicated sanctuaries where native species are brought back from the brink and entire desert, mountain, and riparian ecosystems are restored for future generations.

Ranches vs. Rhode Island

The combined ranches cover about 807 square miles of land. This represents approximately 78% of the entire land area of Rhode Island (1,034 sq mi). Rather than housing over 1 million residents, this territory is dedicated to pristine habitat, bison herds, and endangered species recovery.

Sanctuary Profiles

The Two Anchors

While close geographically, these two properties host vastly different landscapes and biological niches.

Armendaris Ranch
360,000 Acres
Desert Ecosystem

Chihuahuan Desert & Volcanic Flows

Stretching for nearly 50 miles along the historic Rio Grande, the Armendaris Ranch is a landscape of stark, dramatic beauty. It encompasses the entirety of the Fra Cristobal Mountain Range and includes the ancient Jornada lava field.

  • 🏔️ Fra Cristobal Mountains: Home to reintroduced desert bighorn sheep.
  • 🌋 Jornada Lava Tube: Hosts millions of Mexican free-tailed bats in the summer.
  • 🌊 Rio Grande Corridor: A critical migratory flyway for hundreds of bird species.
Ladder Ranch
156,439 Acres
Riparian Ecosystem

Mountain Foothills & Riparian Canyons

Nestled in the eastern foothills of the Black Range, the Ladder Ranch is a rich tapestry of pine forests, desert grasslands, and canyons bisected by four major stream systems: Animas, Seco, Palomas, and Cuchillo.

  • 🌲 Black Range Foothills: Elevation changes that support diverse flora and fauna.
  • 💧 Las Animas Creek: Pure riparian habitat supporting native fish and amphibian programs.
  • 🐺 Mexican Gray Wolf Facility: Premier pre-release site helping wolves adapt to the wild.
Urgency & Pressures

Threats to the Wildlands

Following the passing of Ted Turner, the future of these legacy ranches represents a critical turning point. Without permanent, long-term conservation agreements, these fragile ecosystems face several mounting pressures.

💧

Water Scarcity & Climate Change

The Rio Grande (critical for the Armendaris) and the mountain streams of the Ladder are experiencing severe water stress. Declining snowpacks and prolonged desert droughts dry up crucial riparian corridors, threatening native fish and amphibians like the Chiricahua leopard frog.

🚧

Fragmentation & Subdivision Sprawl

Legacy ranches in the American West are vulnerable to subdivision, commercial sale, and parcel fragmentation. While parts of the Armendaris have conservation easements, the long-term integrity of both ranches requires comprehensive protection to prevent sprawl.

⚡

Infrastructure & Grid Corridors

Proposed energy transmission lines and utility corridors frequently cross southern New Mexico. Cutting these utility paths through pristine grasslands segments wildlife habitats, cutting off migration paths for pronghorn, bison, and bighorn sheep.

A Sanctuary System Worth Securing

The Ladder and Armendaris ranches form a vast, contiguous wildlife corridor that cannot be replicated. Protecting these lands as single-block reserves is one of our most effective buffers against habitat loss, climate stress, and regional biodiversity decline.

Mexican Gray Wolf

25 Wolf Pre-release Capacity

Host of the world's premier pre-release facility, helping recover a population that was once extinct in the wild.

The Recovery Process

Since 1997, the Ladder Ranch has run a specialized captive breeding and pre-release site. The facility helps wolves transition between captivity and the wild, teaching them to pack up and hunt natural prey before they are released into the wild.

Jornada Bat Caves

1.6M+ Summer Bat Population

A subterranean lava tube colony that swells to millions of bats, providing massive insect control for local agriculture.

A Subterranean Sanctuary

Formed by ancient volcanic activity on the Armendaris Ranch, the Jornada lava tubes host one of North America's largest summer colonies of Mexican free-tailed bats. Peak migration numbers can reach up to 5 to 8 million bats, consuming tons of insects nightly.

Bolson Tortoise

800+ Tortoises Under Stewardship

Repatriating North America's largest land tortoise to its prehistoric range after 10,000 years of absence.

Repatriation & Growth

Once native to New Mexico, the Bolson tortoise vanished from the US at the end of the Pleistocene epoch. In 2006, conservationists brought 30 adults from Mexico. Through breeding and juvenile head-start enclosures, the herd has grown to over 800 tortoises.

>80% Juvenile Release Survival

Desert Bighorn Sheep

230+ Mountain Herd Population

The successful recovery of bighorn sheep in the Fra Cristobal range, acting as a source for regional repopulation.

Scaling the Cliffs

Introduced to the isolated Fra Cristobal Mountains on the Armendaris Ranch, this population thrived in the rugged limestone crags. Its dramatic recovery was a key catalyst in state biologists delisting the species from New Mexico's endangered list in 2011.

2011 State Delisting Achieved

Leopard Frog Recovery

20+ Years of Captive Breeding

Rescuing a highly threatened southwestern frog via predator-free breeding tanks and spring releases.

Safe Haven Breeding

To protect threatened Chiricahua leopard frogs from non-native bullfrogs and disease, the Ladder Ranch established a refugium. Biologists raise egg masses in secure outdoor tanks, headstarting thousands of tadpoles and frogs to stock wild creeks and springs.

Tadpoles Main Breeding Target

Rio Grande Cutthroat

Native Las Animas Stream Restoration

Restoring miles of mountain streams to secure populations of New Mexico's state fish.

Restoring Clean Waters

Las Animas Creek on the Ladder Ranch provides the cold, clean water needed by New Mexico’s native cutthroat trout. Conservation efforts involve removing non-native competitors and managing stream banks to support natural spawning and aquatic insect diversity.

🌿 Scale & Species

The massive footprints of the Ladder and Armendaris ranches prove that large-scale habitat protection is one of our most effective tools for preserving biodiversity. By restoring wild stream networks, protecting ancient volcanic caves, and rebuilding self-sustaining animal populations, these New Mexico reserves provide a concrete model for the future of conservation on our planet.
